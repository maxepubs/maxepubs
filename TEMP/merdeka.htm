Merdeka Generation worry about healthcare expenses, say experts

Published
6 hours ago
More
Share Tweet Linkedin Pin Google+ Reddit Print Purchase Article

While they had better opportunities than pioneers, many had jobs that did not pay well

Tham Yuen-C
Senior Political Correspondent
yuenc@sph.com.sg

Born in the 1950s, Singaporeans of the Merdeka Generation did not live through World War II.

Unlike the Pioneer Generation before them, they also had more educational opportunities.

But official statistics show that despite benefiting from an extra decade of economic growth, they did not have the same educational and work opportunities as those born after them.

So while they earned and saved more for retirement than the Pioneer Generation, they still worry about having enough for their healthcare needs, said experts on ageing and Members of Parliament.

In all, Singapore has about 482,200 Merdeka Generation citizens, born between 1950 and 1959, official figures show.

Receptionist Cecilia Ho, 62, who works at the Tsao Foundation, is one of them. She left school after her O levels, spent a year doing a secretarial course and got a job at age 18. She worked her way up from typist to stenographer to secretary.

"I was not in high-paying jobs so I don't think I've saved enough for retirement. If I get a chronic disease, it will wipe out my savings so I've to work and save a bit more," she said.

She has three children, one of whom is still studying. The two who are working do not hold high-paying jobs, she said, adding that they have their own financial responsibilities too.

Like many in her generation, Ms Ho was relieved when Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong said at his recent National Day Rally that a scheme is being worked out to help them with their healthcare expenses.

"The Merdeka Generation Package will go some way to relieve their healthcare worries. More importantly, it will show our appreciation for the Merdeka Generation and their contributions," said PM Lee, who was born in 1952 and belongs to this generation.

"They accepted hardships, made sacrifices, answered the call of duty, and worked with their leaders to build a better tomorrow... I think we owe something to them."

Details of the package will be announced next year. But PM Lee has said it will not be as large as the $8 billion Pioneer Generation Package for Singaporeans.

The comprehensive labour force survey shows that 9 per cent of the post-war baby boomers born between 1948 and 1957 have university degrees. Another 40 per cent have secondary education and 15 per cent have post-secondary education or diplomas and professional qualifications.

In comparison, only 4.3 per cent of those born before 1948 are graduates, while 23 per cent have secondary education and 8 per cent have post-secondary education or diplomas and professional qualifications.

Former Nominated MP Kanwaljit Soin noted that while the Merdeka group had uninterrupted schooling, many cut short their education to work and support their families.

As a result, their educational qualifications were not high, and most had jobs that did not pay well.

Many entered the workforce in the 1970s, a post-independence period of economic uncertainty and high unemployment made worse by the premature withdrawal of the British forces.

The median gross monthly income of full-time employed residents born between 1948 and 1957, including employer CPF contributions, was $2,260 as at June 2017, the survey shows.

The comparative income of those born between 1958 and 1962 was $3,305.

The average Medisave balance of the Merdeka Generation was about $34,000 last year, said a Ministry of Health spokesman.

Jalan Besar GRC MP Lily Neo, who has many Merdeka Generation residents in her constituency, said most held the same type of jobs as those in the Pioneer Generation. As a result, they did not save much for retirement.

The Merdeka Generation women were less educated and earned less than men as well. There were about 237,600 men and 244,600 women in the cohort. But two-thirds of university graduates were men.

In 1965, the average weekly earnings for workmen were $44.55 and for women, about $27.

Dr Soin, founder of Wings which focuses on empowering women to age with confidence, said that the package will go some way to ease the concerns of women in the group.

Said Tsao Foundation's chief executive officer Peh Kim Choo: "Knowing there will be more support for older persons struggling with healthcare bills will be comforting to concerned citizens of all generations."
