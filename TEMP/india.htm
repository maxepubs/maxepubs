WORLD FOCUS

Breathless in India, home to 9 of 10 most polluted cities in the world

1 of 9
123456789

Decades of putting economic growth and infrastructure building first have taken a toll on India's environment. The industrial city of Kanpur illustrates the ill effects of such a policy.

Published: 
Jul 17, 2018, 5:00 am SGT
More
Share Tweet Linkedin Pin Google+ Reddit Print Purchase Article
Nirmala Ganapathy India Bureau Chief In Kanpur

Ms Naseema - who goes by one name - drops wood into a chulha, or traditional wood-fired stove, and fans the flames.

She is cooking lunch - dal (lentils) and roti - as smoke fills her 150 sq ft brick hut on a day with temperatures soaring above 40 deg C. It takes at least two hours and two kilos of wood to cook one meal.

"There is smoke everywhere whenever I cook. My eyes burn and tear up. But what choice do we have?" said the 50-year-old housewife, removing her scarf to reveal blisters on her neck. She and her husband, and 100 other families live in a slum in Jajmau in the suburbs of the industrial city of Kanpur on the banks of the Ganges River.

There are about 100 million people living in slums across India, which has a population of 1.23billion. Like Ms Naseema, they have little choice but to use wood and coal even though they are aware that the resulting pollution is not good for their health.

Smoke from wood and coal-fired ovens is a major source of air pollution in India. A government scheme to connect the poor with liquefied petroleum gas supplies has not yet reached this area, where residents are more worried about where their next meal will come from.

"What other choices do we have? We have to cook with wood," Ms Naseema said when The Straits Times visited recently.

Kanpur topped the world's most polluted cities' list compiled by the World Health Organisation (WHO) in May. India is home to nine of the world's 10 most polluted cities (see infographic), with pollution levels doubling over the past decade due to a lethal combination of factors: rapid industrialisation, an explosion in vehicle numbers, poor public transport system, unchecked construction of homes in urban areas and weak enforcement of laws and regulation.

The second largest city in Uttar Pradesh state, Kanpur is a perfect case in point. Home to three million people, it has more than 1.7 million vehicles, mostly diesel-powered.

The city has 200 working and highly polluting tanneries and dozens of other industrial units.

Set up as an industrial town when India was under British rule, Kanpur was once called the Manchester of the East because of its textile industry, before the factories moved to other parts of country.

The city's crumbling infrastructure is evident on its roads, which are clogged with traffic throughout the day and lined with huge piles of garbage. Open burning of waste is illegal, yet rampant.

Ironically, an ongoing initiative to clean the heavily polluted Ganges made things worse. Dirt roads are being dug up to divert sewer lines to a water treatment plant, but when the vehicles pass by the ditches, they kick up even more dust. "There is a deadly cocktail in the city combined with dust and smoke," said Mr Rakesh Jaiswal,of EcoFriends, a non-profit based in the city. "I have lived here for 25 years and I feel that it has only gotten worse."

HEALTH HAZARDS

WHO's ranking of the world's most polluted cities is based on 2016 levels of particulate matter (PM) 2.5 - pollutants measuring less than 2.5 microns in diameter - about a 30th the diameter of a human hair and so small they can penetrate deep into the lungs, enter the bloodstream and move to other organs.

Kanpur's PM2.5 reading is 173 micrograms per cu m, nearly six times over the WHO's safe limit of 30 micrograms per cu m.

"People are breathing this on a sustained basis. You take one smoke, it harms a little, you take three smokes it harms a little more. It is like you are chain smoking (in Kanpur)," said Professor Sachchida Nand Tripathi of the civil engineering department and Centre for Environmental Science and Engineering at the Indian Institute of Technology in Kanpur.

He said China managed to cut pollution levels by 40 per cent to 50 per cent over a decade starting in 2008. The process has to begin with source apportionment studies to determine the nature of pollution and identify the sources. Yet there is insufficient monitoring of pollutants in India.

"But what is available shows it is very bad, there is no denial," he told The Straits Times.

The city's residents are paying for the high pollution with their health.

Local doctors say they are seeing a spike in respiratory diseases. Dr Prem Singh, head of the department of medicine at Kanpur's Ganesh Shankar Vidyarthi Memorial Medical College, said such afflictions had increased threefold in the past decade.

"In a week we see 300 patients, out of which 100 to 120 have respiratory problems," said Dr Singh.

"What is high is chronic obstructive lung disease, bronchitis, bronchial asthma, allergic, pneumonia. The numbers are increasing."

A recent study by the Centre for Science and Environment found that one in every three children in Delhi has impaired lungs.

"Air pollution causes 30 per cent of all premature deaths in India," it said. According to the WHO, seven million premature deaths - one in eight, or 12.5 per cent, of total global deaths - are linked to air pollution every year.

EFFORTS TO CURB POLLUTION

There is growing awareness across India of the problem as well as mounting public pressure on the government to curb air pollution.

The capital Delhi recently saw its most polluted summer in years, prompting emergency measures, including a halt to all construction activity for days.

Deadly dust storms have become more frequent in recent years.

Pollution control boards and the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change said they are aware of the growing problem and are taking steps such as sprinkling of roads, which are mostly in poor condition, and stepping up the monitoring of industrial emissions.

The Narendra Modi government is finalising the National Clean Air Programme, which aims to unify actions being taken in 29 states, 4,000 cities and towns. For a start, it has identified 100 cities that do not meet air quality standards.

More and more state governments are starting to build metro networks. Kanpur's first metro project, inaugurated in October, 2016 is expected to be completed by 2024.

"The government is very sensitive (to the problem of pollution)," said Mr Kuldeep Misra, regional officer of the Uttar Praedsh Pollution Control Board, adding that the state is looking at setting up a green belt around Kanpur and the construction of peripheral roads to redivert traffic.

Mr Misra also said the city government disagrees with the WHO ranking, although it is true Kanpur is a congested city and that its air quality is bad.

Separately, India, where coal accounts for around half of its energy mix, is looking at increasing the use of clean sources of energy like solar power. Five of the world's largest solar parks are being built in India.

Mr Sunil Dahiya, senior campaigner with Greenpeace India, is heartened by the recent initiatives, saying: "The government has recognised that air pollution is a national crisis and not limited to certain geographies."

But there is a long road ahead.

"Though we talk about clean air targets and standards, there is no compliance mechanism. We have to move in that direction," said Ms Anumita Roychowdhury, executive director at the Centre for Science and Environment.

Until then, there is no respite for police constable Sunil Kumar Yadav who directs traffic in Kanpur.

"All I get (while on duty) are the fumes of trucks and cars. The only time I get relief is when I go home. I do fear for my health," he said.
